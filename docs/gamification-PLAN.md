## The Neuroscience First

Gamification works because of a **two-system brain mechanism**. According to Kent Berridge's research, the brain has a "wanting" system (dopamine) and a "liking" system — and the wanting system is *neurologically stronger*. Apps don't need to make users *satisfied*; they need to make users *crave*. Dopamine spikes hardest not on reward delivery, but on **anticipation of reward** — specifically when that reward is *unpredictable*. That's your entire design philosophy in one sentence: **design for anticipation, not just satisfaction.**[^1_1][^1_2]

Forbes data shows 70% of analysts find traditional dashboards dull, and 50% abandon them for being too complex. SICOP Health is fundamentally a data intelligence dashboard. Without gamification, you're fighting against user apathy every single session.[^1_3]

***

## Why SICOP Health Is a Perfect Gamification Candidate

Your app has a built-in variable reward mechanism that most SaaS tools have to engineer from scratch: **new tenders arrive daily and unpredictably**. This is structurally identical to the slot machine loop — users don't know when the next high-value licitación will drop, which means every app open carries the anticipation of a jackpot. You just need to amplify that signal.[^1_4]

The user's core emotional win is: *"I found a relevant bid before my competitor did."* That's your dopamine trigger. Design everything around surfacing that moment fast, visually, and emotionally.

***

## The 5 Psychological Levers (with Real Data)

### 1. Streaks + Loss Aversion

This is the single highest-ROI mechanic available. Duolingo users who maintained a 7-day streak were **3.6x more likely to stay engaged long-term**, and their Streak Freeze feature alone reduced churn by **21%** for at-risk users. The psychological engine is **loss aversion** — people work harder to avoid losing something they have than to gain something new.[^1_5][^1_6]

**For SICOP Health:** A **"Vigilancia Streak"** — the user checks new tenders daily. Frame it as *"You've monitored 6 days in a row — don't let a licitación slip past you."* The key is the loss framing: you're not promising reward, you're threatening loss of competitive edge.

### 2. Progress Bars + Zeigarnik Effect

LinkedIn's Profile Strength meter drove millions of fully completed profiles using one psychological principle: **we are neurologically wired to hate incomplete things** (the Zeigarnik Effect). Progress bars boost task completion by **40%**.[^1_7][^1_8]

**For SICOP Health:** An **"Intel Score"** or **"Perfil de Monitoreo"** completeness bar. The user needs to: add keywords, select target institutions, set bid-size thresholds, configure alerts. Start them at 40% on signup. Show a persistent bar: *"Your monitoring profile is 40% optimized — you may be missing bids."* Nobody leaves a bar at 40%.

### 3. Variable Reward + Discovery Moments

Unexpected rewards activate the brain's nucleus accumbens **far more intensely** than predictable outcomes. This is the mechanic behind Asana's unicorn celebration on task completion — a tiny, delightful surprise that creates positive emotional anchoring.[^1_9][^1_1]

**For SICOP Health:** When a new bid matching the user's profile hits — don't just show a notification. Trigger a **micro-celebration**: a brief animation, a bold "🔔 Nueva Oportunidad Detectada" modal with confetti or a highlight flash, and a clear CTA. Make it feel like a slot machine paying out. The *moment of discovery* should feel like a win, not a table row update.

### 4. Achievement Badges + Status Identity

HubSpot's Academy badge system drives users to complete certifications and then share them on LinkedIn — turning product engagement into **public identity signals**. SAP uses community mission badges the same way. The mechanism: achievements become part of the user's professional self-concept.[^1_10][^1_9]

**For SICOP Health:** Tiered badges anchored to real behaviors:

- 🏥 **"CCSS Specialist"** — monitored 50+ CCSS bids
- 🔬 **"Health Intel Pro"** — ran 30+ days of streak monitoring
- ⚡ **"First Responder"** — opened a new bid within 1 hour of posting
- 🎯 **"Precision Tracker"** — classifier accuracy feedback contributed 10+ corrections

These badges surface on the user's profile and create **sunk cost identity** — the more badges, the harder it is to churn.[^1_6]

### 5. Urgency Mechanics + FOMO Framing

The dopamine system is especially sensitive to cues that signal a reward is *about to expire*. Timeboxed challenges and deadline framing are proven engagement drivers.[^1_11][^1_2]

**For SICOP Health:** Transform tender deadlines into urgency UI:

- **"⏳ 3 licitaciones cierran en 48 horas"** — a persistent counter on the dashboard
- **"🔥 Oportunidad Caliente"** badge on bids with <72h to closing
- A weekly **"Semana de Oportunidades"** summary email: *"Esta semana: 14 nuevas licitaciones de salud. Tu perfil capturó 6. Configurar alertas para capturar las 8 restantes →"*

## The One Trap to Avoid

Gamification tied to **random actions** (just clicking around) trains hollow behavior and kills intrinsic motivation. Every mechanic you build must reward actions that give the user **real procurement intelligence value**. A streak for *checking the app* is fine. A badge for *filtering bids by UNSPSC code* is better — it deepens their use of the product while feeling rewarding. The reward must align with the user's actual job-to-be-done: *find bids, evaluate them fast, act before competitors do.*[^1_12]

# So i removed the alert system and changed it for a notification inbox

Good call sharing the screenshot — this surfaces a specific issue that would've killed the gamification strategy before it started.

## The Core Architecture Problem

Moving from a push alert system to a pull-based inbox is a **retention downgrade in disguise**. Airship's data on 180 million users shows that apps sending daily push notifications have **820% higher retention rates** than apps sending zero notifications. An inbox requires users to remember to come check — push notifications *interrupt* and create urgency. They're not the same tool.[^2_1][^2_2]

The inbox is fine as a **complement**, but if you killed the push layer entirely, you removed the single highest-ROI retention mechanic available.

***

## What This Screen Is Doing Wrong Right Now

Looking at the screenshot directly, there are 4 concrete problems:

- **The empty state is a dopamine killer.** "Sin notificaciones / No hay nuevas licitaciones médicas en los últimos 30 días" tells the user *nothing is happening*. That's a passive death message. The user opens the tab, gets nothing, associates the tab with nothingness, and stops opening it[^2_3]
- **Nav says "Alertas", page says "Notificaciones"** — cognitive dissonance on the first impression, breaks trust
- **No unread badge counter on the tab** — the red badge/unread dot is one of the most studied engagement mechanics in UX. Apps with badge notifications see up to **88% increase in open rates** via the Zeigarnik Effect (brain compelled to resolve incomplete tasks). Your tab has zero visual state change between "0 notifications" and "5 notifications"[^2_4]
- **30-day lookback for an empty result** — if the classifier is missing bids (which it was at ~32% medical classification rate per your ETL docs ), the user thinks the product doesn't work, not that classification needs tuning[^2_5]

***

## What to Build Instead

### The Inbox Should Have 3 Layers

| Layer | What It Does | Psychological Driver |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **Push notification** (restore) | Interrupts user when a matching bid drops | Variable reward / dopamine spike |
| **Inbox feed** | Persistent history of all matched bids, read/unread state | Zeigarnik Effect / unread compulsion |
| **Badge counter on tab** | Red dot + number on "Alertas" tab | Salience bias + urgency bias |

### Redesign the Empty State Completely

The empty state is not a dead end — it's a **setup prompt dressed as a game quest**. When the inbox is empty, show:

```
🎯 Tu radar está activo
Estás monitoreando: CCSS, Hospital México, INS
Próxima búsqueda: hace 2 min

Licitaciones detectadas hoy en SICOP: 47
Licitaciones de salud para tu perfil: 0

👉 Afina tus palabras clave para capturar más oportunidades →
```

This reframes "nothing found" as **"the radar is watching"** — active anticipation instead of passive emptiness. It shows the system is alive and working, and gives the user an action to improve their results (which also deepens their product usage).

### The Badge Is Non-Negotiable

On the "Alertas" nav tab, you need a live unread count. In Next.js this is a single state update — fetch unread notification count on mount, render a `<span>` badge. When a new bid matches their profile and lands in the inbox, the badge increments. The user sees it, the Zeigarnik loop fires, they tap to clear it. This is how email, Slack, every social platform, and every B2B tool with strong engagement operates.[^2_6][^2_7]

***

## The One Decision to Make Right Now

Do you want the inbox to work as a **passive archive** (user comes to check) or as an **active alert system with history** (push fires → user taps → lands in inbox marked as new)? The second model is what Slack, Linear, and every high-retention B2B tool uses. The inbox alone, without push, is a graveyard — the data is unambiguous on this.[^2_2][^2_1]

# So the system still sends notifications to whatsapp and email and also makes a kind of alert in the app when a new notification is arrived. What changed is user is no longer able to create alerts for specifics. I don’t find it necessary since you can just filter lists and get all notifications alerts from everything without making the user make it himself. I see it as a friction.

Your friction instinct is correct as a general principle — but it runs into a specific problem with SICOP Health's data reality. Let me be direct about what the data says.

## Your Reasoning Is Right, But the Context Breaks It

Removing manual alert builders is standard smart UX. 74% of users abandon apps with complex, demanding setup flows. For a B2C app, "just send everything" works. **But SICOP Health processes ~2,324 tenders per day, of which ~743 are classified as medical**. If your system sends notifications for all of them — WhatsApp messages, emails, in-app pings — you're not reducing friction, you're creating **notification fatigue**, which is one of the hardest retention problems to recover from.[^3_1][^3_2][^3_3]

Users who receive irrelevant or excessive notifications don't adjust their settings — they mute the channel entirely. For WhatsApp specifically, that's a permanent lose: once a user mutes your WA sender, they never unmute it.[^3_4]

## The IKEA Effect You're Leaving on the Table

The research is unambiguous: when users invest effort in configuring something, they develop **psychological ownership** over it and are significantly less likely to churn. The act of setting up a filter isn't friction — it's **the user investing in the product**, which creates stickiness. Tools like Notion, ClickUp, and Linear all leverage this. The question is how you collect that information without it feeling like a form.[^3_5][^3_6]

The answer every high-retention B2B SaaS uses: **onboarding as a conversation, not a config screen**.

## The Right Architecture: Smart Defaults via Onboarding Quiz

Instead of "build your own alert" (friction) or "get everything" (fatigue), do this:

During onboarding, ask **3 questions**, framed as personalization, not configuration:

```
🎯 Vamos a personalizar tu radar de oportunidades

1. ¿Qué tipo de productos vendes o distribuyes?
   → Equipos médicos / Medicamentos / Insumos / Tecnología / Otro

2. ¿A qué instituciones te interesa vender?
   → CCSS / Hospitales nacionales / INS / MS / Todas

3. ¿Cuál es el monto mínimo de licitación que te interesa?
   → ₡5M+ / ₡20M+ / ₡50M+ / Sin filtro
```

That's it. 3 taps. The system now has enough signal to filter from 743 daily medical tenders down to the 5–15 that are actually relevant per user. No alert builder, no technical configuration — but the user still *built* their own radar, which triggers the IKEA Effect.[^3_5]

## What This Does for Gamification

This unlocks the mechanic you couldn't build before:

- **Relevance score on each notification** — *"Este bid coincide 94% con tu perfil"* — which only means something if the user has a profile to match against
- **"Oportunidades perdidas"** stat — *"Esta semana llegaron 3 bids de equipos médicos para CCSS que no abriste"* — creates FOMO and urgency only if the system knows what the user cares about
- **Profile completeness bar** (Intel Score) — gives you a progression mechanic, but only if there's something to complete[^3_7]

Without any user signal, all of these mechanics collapse into noise. The notification inbox becomes a generic feed that means nothing to the user personally, and the gamification layer has nothing to personalize against.

## The Practical Line

| Approach | Friction | Fatigue Risk | Gamification Potential | IKEA Effect |
| :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Manual alert builder (old) | High | Low | High | High |
| Notify everything (current) | Zero | **Very High** | Low | Zero |
| 3-question onboarding quiz | **Minimal** | Low | **High** | **High** |

The 3-question quiz is the move. It takes 20 seconds, it's framed as *"personalizing your experience"* not *"configuring alerts"*, and it gives the entire downstream gamification layer something to operate on.

# I like the quiz thing, what about existing users?

Should we preserve the user ability to create alerts by it's own?

Great questions. Both have clear answers backed by data — no guessing needed.

## Existing Users: Migration, Not Disruption

The cardinal rule of UX migration is **never silently change what a user already configured**. For existing users who had manual alerts set up, yanking that without transition breeds resentment and churn — even if the new system is objectively better. The playbook used by tools like Slack, Linear, and Intercom when deprecating features:[^4_1]

1. **Auto-convert their existing alerts into a pre-filled profile** — if they had an alert for "equipos médicos + CCSS", that becomes their onboarding quiz answer, pre-populated. They see: *"Detectamos que monitoreabas equipos médicos en CCSS — confirmamos tu perfil de inteligencia:"* followed by their 3 answers already filled. One tap to confirm.
2. **Show them what changed and why it benefits them** — *"Antes: alertas manuales. Ahora: tu radar personalizado detecta oportunidades automáticamente."* Frame it as an upgrade, not a removal.
3. **Give new users the quiz, give existing users the confirmation flow** — same destination, different path.[^4_2]

For users with zero prior alerts configured, treat them as new users and send them straight to the quiz.

## Should You Preserve Manual Alert Creation?

The data on this is split depending on user type, and that's the key variable here.

Your users are **procurement professionals and medical suppliers** — not casual consumers. B2B power users specifically exhibit higher engagement and lower churn when they have granular control over their tools. They want to feel like experts using a precision instrument, not passengers being managed by an algorithm.[^4_3]

The risk of removing manual control isn't friction — it's **perceived loss of professional agency**, which in B2B causes quiet churn: the user doesn't complain, they just stop trusting the system and eventually cancel.[^4_4]

The proven model here is what **Braze, HubSpot, and Intercom** all use:

**Smart defaults + power user override**


| Layer | Who It Serves | What It Does |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Onboarding quiz | All new users | Generates smart profile in 3 taps, zero friction |
| Auto-profile confirmation | Existing users | Converts old alerts into new profile format |
| Advanced filters (power mode) | Users who want control | Manual keyword inclusion/exclusion, institution-level toggles, amount thresholds |
| AI suggestions | Passive engagement | *"Detectamos que abres bids de imagenología — ¿agregar al perfil?"* |

The advanced filter section doesn't need to be prominent — it can live behind a *"Personalizar más →"* link at the bottom of the profile screen. Most users will never touch it. Power users will love that it exists. And critically: **the existence of that option increases perceived trust in the system** even for users who never use it, because it signals that the platform respects their judgment.[^4_5][^4_3]

## The Gamification Bonus of Keeping Manual Controls

Letting users add custom keywords creates a **progressive commitment loop** — each keyword they add is an investment in the product, deepening the IKEA Effect. You can reinforce this directly:[^4_6]

*"🎯 Tu radar tiene 4 palabras clave activas — bids encontrados esta semana: 12"*

That number becomes a reward tied to their own configuration effort. The more they customize, the more relevant the results, the more reward they perceive — a self-reinforcing retention engine.[^4_7]

**Bottom line:** Run the quiz as the default path for all users. Preserve advanced manual configuration as an accessible but non-prominent power option. Never make configuration mandatory — make it feel like leveling up.

# <q>IKEA Effect</q> tell me more about this

The IKEA Effect is a formal cognitive bias, not a buzzword. Here's everything you need to know about it — from the science to how it directly applies to SICOP Health.

## The Original Research

The effect was identified by **Michael Norton (Harvard), Daniel Mochon (Tulane), and Dan Ariely (Duke)** in a series of experiments published in the *Journal of Consumer Psychology* in 2012. The name comes from the furniture brand whose entire business model is accidentally the world's largest psychological experiment in self-assembly.[^5_1]

Their core finding: **labor alone causes people to overvalue things they built themselves**, even when the quality is objectively poor. In the original experiment, 52 participants either assembled an IKEA box themselves or received it pre-assembled. The people who built it were willing to pay **63% more** for the exact same product.[^5_2][^5_3]

They also ran the same experiment with origami and Lego sets — same result across all three.[^5_4]

## The Three Underlying Mechanisms

The paper identifies why this happens at a psychological level:[^5_1]

- **Effectance (competence)** — humans have a fundamental need to produce desired outcomes in their environment. Completing a task successfully triggers a real neurological reward, and the created object becomes associated with that feeling
- **Psychological ownership** — the effort invested makes the brain treat the object as an extension of the self. Discarding it feels like self-loss
- **Justification of effort** — the brain rationalizes that something you worked hard on *must* be valuable, otherwise the effort was wasted (related to cognitive dissonance)


## The Critical Boundary Condition

This is the part most people miss: **the effect only activates when the task is successfully completed**. Norton's experiments showed that when participants built something and then had to destroy it, or were stopped before finishing, the IKEA Effect completely disappeared — willingness-to-pay dropped back to baseline.[^5_5][^5_4]

This has a direct design implication: **any setup flow you design must reach a satisfying completion state**. A half-finished onboarding quiz, a profile bar stuck at 40% with no obvious next step, a keyword configuration that never confirms — all of these *destroy* the IKEA Effect instead of triggering it. The user has to cross the finish line and *know* they crossed it.

## How It Works in Digital Products

The most successful SaaS products today deliberately engineer this effect:[^5_6]

- **Notion** — users build their entire workspace from scratch. Every page, database, and template they construct becomes theirs. Notion's retention is famously tied to how much the user has built, not how long they've been subscribed
- **Spotify** — Wrapped, playlists, and listening habit summaries reflect the user's own behavior back at them. The product becomes a mirror of their taste, which they perceive as *their creation*
- **iOS/Android** — deep personalization options make devices feel uniquely owned, which is why people are emotionally reluctant to switch phones even when competitors are objectively better[^5_7]


## How This Maps Directly to SICOP Health

Your 3-question onboarding quiz isn't just reducing friction — it's **engineering a completion event** that activates the IKEA Effect. When the user finishes it and sees:

```
✅ Tu radar de inteligencia está configurado
Monitoreando: Equipos médicos · CCSS · ₡20M+
```

...that dashboard is now *theirs*. They built it. The classifier results it surfaces feel like *their intelligence*, not the platform's. That psychological shift is the difference between a tool people use and a tool people protect.[^5_8]

The keywords they add later, the institutions they toggle, the filters they save — each one is another IKEA box assembled. Each completed action deepens ownership, and **each deepened ownership reduces the probability of churn** because leaving the product means abandoning something the user built, which the brain treats as self-abandonment.[^5_6]

The practical rule for your product: **never let the user feel like a passenger**. Every interaction where they make a meaningful choice is an opportunity to trigger this effect. Keep those choices achievable (clear completion state), meaningful (tied to real results), and visible (show them what their effort produced).
